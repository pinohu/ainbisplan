# Per-Business Authority Plan Contract

**Status:** Required  
**Applies to:** every AINBIS inventory record

## Purpose

The shared platform supplies reusable capabilities, but every business must have a specific plan. The plan is a versioned contract between the blueprint, research, design system, content operation, implementation, launch network, and runtime.

A generic niche label is not sufficient. The plan must be based on the actual blueprint, intended customer, workflow, jurisdiction, source material, commercial model, and overlap with the rest of the portfolio.

## Required plan identity

Each plan records:

- inventory slug and normalized title;
- brand or product name;
- source repository and blueprint path;
- blueprint content hash and plan generator version;
- source classification and developed/launched status;
- planning status and indexability state;
- primary and secondary archetypes;
- owner, editorial owner, technical owner, domain reviewer, and approver;
- created, reviewed, approved, implemented, launched, and next-review dates;
- superseded plans and migration history.

## Required business understanding

The plan must describe:

- the business’s independent reason to exist;
- audience segments and buying committee;
- primary and secondary jobs to be done;
- triggering events, urgency, frequency, and consequences;
- existing alternatives and why users would switch;
- product, service, workflow, advisory, content, data, directory, or hybrid model;
- deliverables and customer outcomes;
- boundaries, exclusions, prerequisites, and escalation routes;
- pricing posture and conversion model;
- jurisdiction, geography, language, and service availability;
- regulatory, legal, clinical, financial, environmental, safety, employment, housing, children’s, identity, or other sensitive-domain classification;
- YMYL level and required review intensity;
- evidence needed to support claims and conversion promises.

## Authority thesis

Each business must answer:

1. What narrowly defined subject should this site become exceptionally useful for?
2. Who should trust it, and why?
3. What original value will exist that cannot be produced by superficial rewriting?
4. What first-hand experience, data, tools, workflows, methodology, or expert judgment supports that value?
5. What adjacent subjects are explicitly outside the site’s focus?
6. Which AINBIS businesses overlap, and how is cannibalization avoided?
7. Should the business remain independent, be repositioned, be consolidated, or remain non-indexable?

## Research packet

No business becomes an indexable candidate without a research packet containing, as applicable:

- primary laws, regulations, standards, agency guidance, official forms, deadlines, and definitions;
- authoritative professional, academic, technical, clinical, or industry sources;
- current search landscape and user-language research;
- audience interviews, practitioner input, customer evidence, or documented first-hand experience;
- competitor and alternative analysis;
- common errors, failure modes, disputes, and misconceptions;
- process, document, data, and decision requirements;
- jurisdiction and effective-date matrix;
- source freshness, archival copy, access date, and reviewer notes;
- unresolved questions and explicit research gaps.

Sensitive businesses require qualified domain review before claims, guidance, calculators, templates, or generated documents are approved.

## Search and entity plan

Each plan defines:

- topic boundary and primary topic cluster;
- query and intent universe;
- primary entities, identifiers, attributes, and relationships;
- regulatory and standards graph;
- problem, role, industry, organization-type, process, document, deadline, and location dimensions;
- query-to-page map;
- unique page purpose and indexability for every proposed URL;
- competitor and search-result gaps that the business can credibly satisfy;
- content cannibalization and portfolio collision assessment;
- schema eligibility by page type;
- internal and justified cross-site relationships.

## Information architecture

Every plan includes a proposed URL and navigation map with:

- homepage purpose and primary action;
- product or service pages;
- solution and audience pages;
- authority hubs and supporting resources;
- tool, calculator, assessment, template, and download sections;
- expert, methodology, source, policy, and trust pages;
- pricing, process, eligibility, security, privacy, support, and contact pages;
- glossary and question architecture;
- local or jurisdiction pages only when independently useful;
- archive, filter, search, pagination, and parameter behavior;
- breadcrumb and internal-link hierarchy;
- launch, later-wave, consolidation, and excluded URLs.

## Content portfolio

The plan must specify:

- foundational evergreen pages;
- regulatory or standards guides;
- workflow and implementation guides;
- decision-support content;
- comparisons and alternatives;
- questions and definitions;
- templates and examples;
- original data, benchmark, research, or methodology opportunities;
- case studies or scenarios and evidence requirements;
- timely update categories and change triggers;
- newsletter editorial pillars;
- content ownership, reviewer, review interval, and retirement rule;
- initial launch corpus and expansion backlog;
- prohibited content that would be thin, duplicative, unsupported, or outside the business purpose.

## Original utility plan

Every business must identify at least one defensible utility path. Depending on the niche, this can include:

- readiness or compliance assessment;
- eligibility checker;
- deadline or obligation tracker;
- calculator or estimator;
- document completeness checker;
- guided workflow;
- template or evidence pack;
- comparison or decision matrix;
- audit workbook;
- benchmark or dataset;
- interactive regulation or process map;
- personalized report with disclosed assumptions.

The plan records inputs, outputs, formulas or logic, source basis, update triggers, accessibility requirements, risks, disclaimers, review requirements, data handling, analytics, conversion role, and maintenance owner.

## Lead capture and lifecycle plan

Each plan defines:

- ungated value that proves usefulness;
- optional gated resources tied to visitor intent;
- primary and secondary conversion paths;
- form fields and minimum necessary data;
- lead qualification, routing, ownership, and response expectations;
- consent and privacy basis;
- newsletter decision and audience promise;
- segmentation and preference options;
- welcome, nurture, deadline, education, and re-engagement sequences;
- CRM or portal integration;
- conversion-quality feedback and attribution.

## Trust and evidence plan

Every business specifies:

- organization identity and ownership disclosure;
- author and reviewer requirements;
- expert advisory needs;
- source hierarchy;
- citation and methodology requirements;
- credentials, licenses, certifications, affiliations, and verification method;
- client proof, case studies, reviews, and permission rules;
- proof placeholders for pre-launch sites that cannot fabricate evidence;
- claim taxonomy and required substantiation;
- limitations, disclaimers, and professional-advice boundaries;
- corrections, complaints, appeals, and incident routes;
- AI and automation disclosure requirements.

## Technical implementation profile

The plan chooses and configures:

- rendering strategy;
- hosting and deployment target;
- URL, canonical, redirect, robots, sitemap, and indexability rules;
- schema types and entity identifiers;
- image, video, downloadable-file, and media handling;
- Core Web Vitals and resource budgets;
- accessibility requirements beyond the shared minimum;
- localization and hreflang needs;
- security, privacy, consent, data retention, and upload requirements;
- analytics events, attribution, dashboards, and experiments;
- monitoring, alerts, backup, rollback, and incident response;
- integration dependencies and operational owners.

## Off-site authority plan

Each business identifies credible methods for earning recognition:

- original studies, benchmarks, or reference materials;
- expert commentary and media participation;
- professional associations and standards communities;
- academic, practitioner, vendor, nonprofit, government, or industry partnerships;
- webinars, events, podcasts, newsletters, and conferences;
- legitimate directories and business listings;
- review and reputation strategy;
- unlinked mention and backlink monitoring;
- community contribution and outreach;
- prohibited acquisition tactics.

## Implementation backlog

The plan decomposes work into epics:

1. research and differentiation;
2. business and offer validation;
3. brand and archetype configuration;
4. content and entity models;
5. information architecture;
6. authority UI and page compositions;
7. core product or service workflow;
8. original tools and resources;
9. editorial and expert-review operations;
10. technical SEO and structured data;
11. internal linking and content intelligence;
12. lead capture, newsletter, CRM, and lifecycle;
13. analytics, attribution, and experimentation;
14. accessibility, performance, security, and privacy;
15. off-site authority and reputation;
16. migration, launch, indexation, monitoring, and continuous improvement.

Every epic records deliverables, dependencies, owner, review role, acceptance criteria, evidence, status, and blockers.

## Generation and review workflow

For every inventory record:

1. ingest inventory metadata;
2. locate and parse the blueprint;
3. extract audience, problem, offer, workflow, jurisdictions, sources, claims, and risks;
4. classify archetypes and sensitivity;
5. compare the business with all existing portfolio plans;
6. identify overlap, collisions, and potential consolidation;
7. generate a plan scaffold using the schema;
8. conduct current primary-source and search-landscape research;
9. add business-specific authority, content, utility, conversion, technical, and off-site decisions;
10. assign domain and editorial review;
11. validate schema and mandatory gates;
12. commit the plan and update the portfolio index;
13. create implementation issues or epics;
14. keep plan, source inventory, catalog, build, and runtime states synchronized.

The generator may scaffold a plan. It may not mark research, expert review, differentiation, or indexability gates as passed without evidence.

## File convention

Individual plans are generated to:

```text
plans/businesses/<first-character>/<slug>/
├── plan.json
├── plan.md
├── research-manifest.json
├── content-map.json
├── entity-graph.json
├── implementation-backlog.json
└── evidence/
```

The machine-readable `plan.json` is authoritative. Markdown is a generated review view. Supporting documents must reference the same `plan_id`, business slug, blueprint hash, and plan version.

## Continuous synchronization

A plan is stale when:

- the blueprint hash changes;
- inventory title, status, or source changes;
- a regulation, standard, source, or jurisdiction changes;
- a sibling business creates a topic or query collision;
- a product, service, offer, audience, geography, reviewer, or owner changes;
- a site migration changes URLs or canonical relationships;
- monitoring identifies quality, policy, performance, accessibility, security, or reputation risk.

Stale plans cannot advance to indexable status until the relevant sections are reviewed.
